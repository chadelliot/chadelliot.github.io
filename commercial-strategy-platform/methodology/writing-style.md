# Writing Style

Use this guide when turning research, Commercial Intelligence, and an Opportunity Model into generated output copy.

The writing should feel executive, direct, directional, data-driven, practical, confident, and curious. It should avoid generic marketing language, never overstate what is known, and preserve the current Commercial Strategy page's concise visual rhythm when generating website or proposal-style outputs.

## Voice

Write like a commercial operator briefing an executive team.

Preferred qualities:

- executive
- direct
- directional
- data-driven
- specific
- practical
- commercially literate
- measured
- confident without overclaiming
- curious about what internal data will prove or disprove

Avoid:

- overstating public research
- hype
- buzzwords without operational meaning
- vague transformation language
- generic praise for the company
- consultant filler
- long paragraphs that will not fit the design

## Non-Negotiables

- Never pretend to know internal priorities.
- Never imply access to internal performance data, strategy documents, customer data, pipeline data, budget, or systems unless explicitly provided.
- Never overstate what public research proves.
- Always separate sourced facts, directional hypotheses, and recommendations.
- Always acknowledge that the strategy should evolve after learning internal constraints.
- Use confidence for the recommendation, not for unsupported facts.

## Audience

The primary reader is an executive or hiring leader evaluating strategic judgment.

The output should be framed by one of three engagement types: Executive Hire, Consulting Engagement, or Agency Engagement. The deliverable may be a strategy page, proposal, executive dashboard, interview preparation brief, cover letter, or LinkedIn/recruiter outreach.

Assume the reader wants to know:

- whether the company context is understood
- whether the strategy is specific to their market
- how the audience and revenue opportunity will be prioritized
- how commercial activations and sales motions become executable
- how success will be measured
- what should happen over the next 12-24 months

## Sentence Style

Prefer short, decisive sentences.

Use active voice:

- "Prioritize enterprise operators with multi-site complexity."
- "Use leadership hiring as an expansion signal."
- "Measure pipeline quality before activation volume."

Avoid soft, padded phrasing:

- "It may be beneficial to consider..."
- "There is an opportunity to potentially..."
- "A robust and comprehensive approach would..."

## Specificity Standard

Every major claim should connect to at least one of:

- job description language
- company research
- public leadership priority
- customer segment
- market condition
- revenue signal
- buying committee need
- operational recommendation

Weak:

```text
The company should improve marketing and sales alignment.
```

Stronger:

```text
Align marketing and sales around Tier 1 account movement, signal response time, and sales-accepted pipeline from priority segments.
```

## Handling Inference

Do not present inferred facts as sourced facts.

Use cautious framing when the research supports a hypothesis but not a confirmed fact:

- "appears"
- "suggests"
- "likely"
- "public signals indicate"
- "assumption to validate"

Use direct framing for recommendations:

- "Prioritize..."
- "Measure..."
- "Build..."
- "Separate..."
- "Use..."

## Internal Priorities And Constraints

Public research can support a strong directional strategy, but it cannot fully reveal internal priorities or constraints.

When internal context is unknown, write with disciplined humility:

- "Public signals suggest..."
- "A directional starting point is..."
- "This should be validated against internal pipeline data."
- "The roadmap should evolve once internal constraints are known."
- "Initial priorities should be adjusted after reviewing current systems, budget, team capacity, and sales coverage."

Avoid:

- "The company's top priority is..."
- "Leadership is focused on..."
- "The internal constraint is..."
- "The team should immediately..."

Use public leadership priorities only when they are visible in sourced public material. Otherwise, frame priorities as hypotheses or recommended focus areas.

## Section Style

### KPI Foundation

Write objectives as business outcomes, not activities.

Prefer:

- "Increase qualified pipeline from priority enterprise accounts."
- "Improve forecast confidence by separating demand creation from opportunity acceptance."

Avoid:

- "Run more activation plays."
- "Create better dashboards."

### Audience Architecture

Make segments and personas distinct.

Each segment should explain:

- who it is
- why it matters
- what pain or priority makes it relevant
- how the motion should differ

Each persona should include a real decision lens, not a generic title description.

### Total Addressable Market

Be clear about assumptions.

Use market sizing to support prioritization, not to impress with unsupported numbers.

Prefer:

- "Estimated reachable opportunity based on priority verticals and regional footprint."
- "Assumption to validate: multi-site operators represent the highest near-term conversion pool."

### Prospect Funnel

Translate market intelligence into funnel prioritization.

Each insight should answer:

- what changed
- why it matters
- what marketing or sales should do next

### Signal Intelligence

Write from the buyer's perspective.

Each journey stage should include:

- the buyer question
- the proof needed
- the commercial action that moves the account forward

Signals should also remain observable and actionable.

Avoid vague signals like "interest" or "engagement" unless the behavior is defined.

Prefer:

- "New executive hire in operations"
- "Acquisition integration announcement"
- "Expansion into a new region"
- "Hiring for revenue operations or field sales"

### Commercial Activation

Commercial activations should have a specific audience, offer, channel, investment, pipeline, revenue, and ROI logic.

Avoid activation names that sound like internal slogans unless they clarify the buyer value.

Prefer language that helps a VP decide where to invest, which accounts to prioritize, and what return to expect.

### Sales Motion

Sales plays should be operational.

Include:

- entry point
- owner
- stakeholders
- assets
- exit criteria

### Return to Dashboard

Dashboard copy should help leadership inspect progress.

Prefer metrics that show movement through the strategy:

- priority account engagement
- signal response time
- sales-accepted pipeline
- opportunity conversion
- expansion pipeline

### M+S Alignment

Measurement should distinguish activity from outcomes.

Use leading indicators and lagging indicators intentionally.

### System Evolution

System evolution copy should acknowledge that strategy changes after internal constraints are learned.

Use practical sequencing when needed:

- 0-90 days
- 3-6 months
- 6-12 months
- 12-24 months

Keep this as supporting copy inside the existing sections, not as a new page section.

## Formatting For The Existing Design

The current Commercial Strategy design works best with compact copy. Apply these limits to website and proposal-style Renderer outputs.

Guidelines:

- labels: 1-4 words
- card titles: 2-7 words
- card body text: 1-2 short sentences
- bullets: short phrases where possible
- stage summaries: one concise sentence
- dashboard questions: short enough to scan quickly

Avoid:

- multi-paragraph card bodies
- nested lists inside content fields
- very long labels
- repeated organization-name insertion
- copy that requires a new layout to fit

## Terms To Prefer

Use language that fits commercial strategy:

- audience architecture
- priority segments
- buying committee
- revenue intelligence
- signal-based activation
- sales-accepted pipeline
- executive dashboard
- measurement cadence
- operating rhythm
- roadmap horizons

## Terms To Use Carefully

Use these only when they are specific and useful:

- transformation
- AI-powered
- best-in-class
- end-to-end
- scalable
- frictionless
- omnichannel
- personalization
- data-driven

If one of these terms appears, the sentence should explain what it means operationally.

## Quality Bar

Before finalizing generated copy, confirm:

- the output would not work unchanged for a different opportunity
- every section or artifact has at least one opportunity-specific insight
- recommendations are tied to business outcomes
- copy is concise enough for the selected output format
- assumptions are clearly marked
- sourced facts, directional hypotheses, and recommendations are distinct
- the copy does not pretend to know internal priorities
- the strategy acknowledges that it should evolve after learning internal constraints
- metrics are measurable
- the roadmap is sequenced, not just a list of ideas
