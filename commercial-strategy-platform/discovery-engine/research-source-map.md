# Research Source Map

This file maps the Discovery Engine areas to the most useful source types.

The goal is not to exhaust every possible source. The goal is to find the smallest set of sources that can support confident Commercial Intelligence.

## Source Hierarchy

### External Sources

- company website
- product or service pages
- about or leadership pages
- investor relations pages
- annual reports
- earnings transcripts
- press releases
- newsroom posts
- competitor websites
- LinkedIn company pages
- public employee profiles
- job postings
- review sites
- technology signals or public pixels
- third-party market research
- public customer case studies

### Internal Or Provided Sources

- job description URL
- job description text
- recruiter notes
- hiring manager notes
- proposal brief
- client briefing notes
- known objectives
- known constraints
- prior strategy notes
- conversation history

## Discovery Area Map

| Discovery area | Primary external sources | Internal or provided sources | What to capture | Confidence signal |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Company overview | About page, leadership page, newsroom, investor materials | Intake notes, recruiter notes | What the company does, who it serves, and the basic operating model | Strong when multiple sources align |
| Products/services | Product pages, case studies, demos, pricing pages, competitor comparisons | Job description, client notes | Main offerings, package structure, and positioning | Strong when offer pages are explicit |
| Industry | Website language, investor materials, analyst context, competitor sites | Job description, notes | Market category, vertical, and operating context | Strong when category language is repeated |
| Competitors | Comparison pages, analyst lists, search results, public market context | Hiring manager notes, client notes | Most relevant competitors and substitutes | Strong when company names recur across sources |
| Customers | Case studies, logos, testimonials, LinkedIn posts, market pages | Intake notes, brief | Customer segments, account types, and buying groups | Strong when segments are named directly |
| Business model | Pricing pages, product packaging, investor materials, partner pages | Notes, proposal brief | How the company creates value and delivers the offer | Strong when monetization is stated openly |
| Revenue model | Pricing, subscription language, contract language, investor materials | Executive context, notes | Recurring, transactional, services, usage, or hybrid model | Strong when monetization type is public |
| Hiring context | Job description, career pages, org charts, leadership posts | Intake object, recruiter notes | Why the role or engagement exists now | Strong when the posting states outcomes |
| Marketing maturity | Campaign pages, newsletter cadence, ad library, CRM/public stack clues | Client notes, proposal brief | How developed the marketing engine appears to be | Strong when multiple signals point the same way |
| Technology signals | Tech stack pages, public pixels, browser headers, job posts, product pages | Internal notes, technical brief | Likely systems, stack patterns, and tooling gaps | Strong when tech is named directly |
| Growth signals | Press, hiring, funding, product launches, expansion posts | Notes, timeline context | Where growth pressure or expansion is visible | Strong when growth events are recent and repeated |
| Risks | Public incidents, reviews, market pressure, competitor moves, regulatory context | Internal notes, sponsor concerns | Commercial, operational, or reputational risks | Strong when risk is observable and current |

## Evidence Rules

- Prefer source links over paraphrase when the claim is material.
- Prefer multiple sources when a claim affects strategy.
- Mark weakly supported claims as inferred or assumption-level.
- Do not use one source to overstate a broad conclusion.
- If no source is available, leave the finding open and lower confidence.
