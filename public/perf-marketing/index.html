Chapter 02 — Segmentation

Know the audience. Define the growth.

In a consumer business, not all customers are equal — in value, in payback speed, or in the channels that reach them. Segmentation is how we stop treating a broad market as one audience. It tells us who our highest-value customers are, what they look like, where they come from, and which prospect profiles are most likely to convert and retain. The segments define the growth objective: who to acquire more of, who to expand, and who is too expensive to chase.

"Segmentation is not a targeting exercise. It is a business decision. It tells us which customers fund the model, which ones drain it, and where the next dollar of paid media should go."
What segmentation reveals
Which customer profiles have the lowest CAC and highest LTV
Which acquisition channels source the best-retaining customers
Where the largest untapped lookalike opportunity sits
Which segments are over-indexed on spend relative to return
Where creative and message needs to change by audience type
Growth objectives that follow
Scale acquisition of highest-LTV customer profiles
Reduce blended CAC by shifting budget to efficient segments
Expand affiliate and partnership reach into new audience pools
Improve payback period by prioritizing fast-converting segments
Reduce churn by matching retention programs to segment behavior

Chapter 04 — Audience Architecture

Who gets the message. And what it says.

We structure the audience in two layers. First, we tier consumers by actual or estimated lifetime value — this tells us where to concentrate paid media investment and where to build lookalike scale. Then within each tier, we define the personas who make the purchase decision, based on their job context, their pain point, and what message breaks through for them specifically.

"Tier tells us where the media dollar belongs. Persona tells us what the creative needs to say to the person seeing it."

Chapter 06 — Signal Intelligence

Act on the right signal at the right moment.

In performance marketing, signals are the competitive edge. The brands winning on Meta and Google in 2025 are not the ones with the biggest budgets — they are the ones feeding the algorithms the right data, reading intent at the account and contact level, and acting before competitors do. Signals tell us when to scale, when to pull back, and when to launch.

"A signal is a reason to act now. Intent data, funding events, lifecycle behavior, platform engagement — each one is an opportunity to reach the right person at exactly the right moment with exactly the right message."

Chapter 07 — Campaign Activation

Chapter 08 — Channel Strategy

The right channel for every stage of growth.

Channel diversification is not about being everywhere — it is about having the right acquisition engine at each stage of the funnel and the growth curve. We manage Meta and Google as the core performance layer, build affiliate as an owned efficiency channel, and use TikTok and CTV to expand the top-of-funnel addressable market without cannibalizing paid performance.

"The goal is a channel mix where no single platform failure can break the acquisition model. Diversification is a risk management strategy as much as a growth strategy."

Chapter 09 — Measurement & Attribution

Measure what actually drives growth.

Post-iOS 14, platform-reported metrics are directional at best. We run a measurement stack that combines server-side tracking via Meta CAPI, incrementality testing for true channel lift, and blended CAC as the primary decision metric — not ROAS from a platform dashboard. This is what separates teams that actually know what's working from teams that think they do.

"Platform ROAS is not a business metric. It is a signal. We make budget and channel decisions on blended CAC, LTV by cohort and source, incrementality-validated lift, and CAC payback period. Those are the numbers that tell the truth."
