Chapter 02 — Segmentation

Know the saver. Define the growth.

Gainbridge's core customer is an everyday and retirement saver, many of them 45+, who wants control over their financial future without the complexity, hidden fees, or agent pressure of traditional annuity providers. But not all savers are equal in value, in acquisition cost, or in the channels that reach them effectively. Segmentation tells us who to prioritize, how to message each group, and where growth is still available in the market.

"Segmentation is not a targeting exercise. It tells us which customers fund the business and which ones don't — and it defines the growth objective for every channel and campaign that follows."

Chapter 03 — Total Addressable Market

Map the retirement savings opportunity.

The US annuity market is large and growing — driven by an aging population, rising interest rates making fixed annuities more attractive, and a generation of savers who are increasingly skeptical of traditional financial advisors. Chapter 04 — Audience Architecture

Who gets the message. And what it says.

We build the audience in two layers. First, we tier savers by actual or estimated deposit value — this establishes where media investment should be concentrated and where the Haus measurement framework should be most rigorously applied. Then within each tier, we define the personas who make the decision to open a Gainbridge account, based on their financial context, their anxiety, and what message breaks through the trust barrier unique to financial services.

"Financial products require a higher trust threshold than almost any other consumer category. Messaging must meet the saver where they are — their fear, their aspiration, their skepticism — not where the product team wants them to be."

Chapter 06 — Signal Intelligence

Read the market. Act before competitors do.

In a regulated financial services category, signals are particularly powerful because most competitors are slow to respond to them. Interest rate movements, competitor product changes, macroeconomic anxiety events, and media spend shifts all create windows where the right message at the right moment dramatically improves acquisition efficiency. Building a signal monitoring practice is a competitive advantage the MMM framework is designed to capture.

"The Fed moves rates. Consumers get anxious. Search volume for 'guaranteed savings' spikes. Most brands respond in weeks. We respond in hours. That window is where the CPA advantage is built."

Chapter 08 — Martech & Team

Build the infrastructure the brand demands.

The VP role explicitly calls for a Martech strategy — evaluate what exists, decide what stays, what gets enhanced, what gets replaced, and partner with engineering to implement it. Alongside that, inheriting the existing team means an honest assessment of composition, capabilities, and gaps, followed by a staffing plan that positions marketing for what comes next. Both are foundational. Neither can wait.

"The Martech stack is the operating system of the marketing function. If it can't track, attribute, or activate at the speed the business requires, every other investment is working at a disadvantage."
